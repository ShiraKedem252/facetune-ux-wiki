# Persona: Gen X User (46-60)

Gen X is the "bridge generation" — raised analog, living digital. They have the highest median household income of any generation ($101K), own an average of 7 devices, and are present on every major social platform. But they use social media fundamentally differently from younger cohorts: it's about family connection, not personal branding.

## The Core Motivation

Gen X users want to look like themselves on a good day. Not younger. Not different. Not transformed. Just fresh and natural — a version of themselves that people will still recognize. This aligns with the [[authenticity-and-editing]] tension seen across all segments, but for Gen X the concern isn't about peer detection (as with [[detection-anxiety]] in males or [[persona-zoe-gen-z-selective]]). It's about trust — if the results look fake, they lose faith in the tool entirely.

## Key Behaviors

- **Family-first sharing:** 51% use social media primarily for family. Photos are of events, vacations, milestones. They're not curating a personal brand — they're documenting life for the people who matter.
- **Platform preferences:** YouTube (85%) and Facebook (74%) dominate. Instagram at 40%, TikTok at 30%. Facebook is used daily by 54%.
- **Control-seeking:** They value the intensity slider — the ability to dial an edit up or down. They don't want all-or-nothing filters.
- **Selective editing:** Like Zoe (Gen Z selective), they prefer targeted, subtle edits. Unlike Zoe, there's no guilt or social stigma — just a practical desire for natural results.
- **Low tolerance for complexity:** The editor menu feels too big and too complex. Finding the same feature in multiple places creates confusion. They want fewer tools, better organized.

## Pain Points

- **Accessibility barriers (high):** Small text, low contrast, light UI, small tap targets, and precise gesture requirements all create friction. This isn't "nice to fix" — it's blocking adoption.
- **Cognitive overload:** Complex navigation and hidden features cause confusion. A packed menu with duplicate tools makes the app feel harder than the editing itself.
- **Trust and privacy:** AI skepticism and data use worries. They need to understand what the app is doing with their photos.
- **Fake-looking results break trust:** Unlike younger users who fear peer detection, Gen X users simply reject results that don't look like them. The threshold for "too much" is lower.

## Comparison to Other Segments

- **vs. Nicole (Beauty-Driven Millennial):** Nicole edits confidently for beauty enhancement. Gen X edits conservatively for natural preservation.
- **vs. Zoe (Gen-Z Selective):** Both prefer subtle edits, but Zoe's restraint is driven by peer judgment and authenticity anxiety. Gen X's restraint is driven by practical preference — they just don't want to look different.
- **vs. Rachel (Family Photographer):** Strong overlap — both are family-focused. Rachel is already in the user base (9%). Gen X may be a growth opportunity that extends Rachel's segment to an older demographic.
- **vs. Annie (Memory Documenter):** Similar "preservation" motivation. Annie edits to preserve memories; Gen X edits to look good in family photos. Both are low-drama, practical editors.

## Retention & Growth Opportunities

- **Age-appropriate "Looks" presets:** Create presets that feel subtle and natural for this age group. Results that look fake break trust instantly.
- **Simplified menu for older users:** Surface only the most-used tools. Remove duplicate feature placements. Larger tap targets, higher contrast text.
- **Accessibility investment:** This benefits all users, not just Gen X. Larger text, better contrast, fewer precise gestures needed.
- **Family sharing integration:** Since 51% share with family, make sharing to Facebook and messaging apps seamless.
- **Earning power:** $101K median household income — highest of any generation. They can afford a premium subscription if the value is clear.

- **Limitations:** Small usability sample; self-selected Facetune users only
