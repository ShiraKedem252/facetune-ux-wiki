# Persona: Rachel — Family Photographer

Rachel is the smallest and most specialized segment — the family photographer who uses Facetune to touch up photos of others, not selfies. She represents **9% of the user base** but is distinctive enough to deserve focused attention.

## The Core Motivation

Rachel is the person who gets handed the family camera or asked to photograph an event. She's often semi-professional (not a paid photographer, but the "good photographer" in the family or friend group). She uses Facetune to clean up photos after the shoot — fixing blemishes on other people, smoothing skin, adjusting lighting. For Rachel, Facetune is a post-production tool, not a social media filter.

## Key Behaviors

- **Edits others' photos primarily**: Rachel is more likely to edit photos of family members, friends, or event attendees than her own selfies. She's thinking about making others look good, not herself.
- **Touch-up focused**: Rachel applies subtle, professional-looking edits. She removes blemishes, smooths skin, adjusts lighting — but aims for natural results.
- **Higher volume editing**: Rachel edits many photos from an event (a family gathering, wedding, kids' school event). She's a batch editor like Annie, but with a slightly different motivation.
- **Quality consciousness**: Rachel cares that photos look professional. She might compare her edits to what a photographer would do and tries to match that standard.
- **Event-driven usage**: Rachel's editing is tied to events or occasions — family holidays, birthday parties, school events. Usage spikes around these moments.

## Pain Points

- **Limited feature set for her use case**: Rachel needs portrait retouching tools (blemish removal, skin smoothing, lighting adjustment), but doesn't need creative effects or body reshaping. Facetune has all three, but doesn't prioritize the ones Rachel needs.
- **Skin tone accuracy**: If Rachel is editing photos of people with different skin tones, Facetune's one-size-fits-all skin smoothing might not work well. Color grading and tone matching are hard.
- **Workflow inefficiency**: Rachel might want to apply the same lighting adjustment to 15 photos from an event. Doing it one by one is time-consuming.
- **Too much emphasis on selfies**: Facetune's UI, help docs, and examples are all about self-editing. Rachel feels like the app wasn't designed for her use case.
- **Awkwardness of editing others**: Rachel might feel weird about heavily editing other people's appearances. Does she have consent? Facetune frames editing as self-enhancement, not touch-up work.

## Comparison to Other Segments

- **vs. Annie (Memory Documenter)**: Both batch-edit photos from events, but Annie is family-focused and preservation-focused. Rachel has a slight professional lens and edits more for presentation.
- **vs. Nicole (Beauty-Driven Millennial)**: Nicole edits selfies for social. Rachel rarely edits her own photo. Different use case entirely.
- **vs. Alex (Influencer-Business)**: Alex uses editing as a professional income driver. Rachel is semi-professional, amateur, or hobbyist.
- **vs. Other segments**: Rachel is unique in her focus on editing others' photos rather than her own. Most other personas are self-focused.

## Retention & Growth Opportunities

- **Highlight touch-up workflows**: Show Rachel examples of subtle, professional edits. Case studies of retouching work from small photographers or family documentarians.
- **Batch editing tools**: If Rachel can apply edits to multiple photos at once, she becomes a power user. This is critical for retention.
- **Tone and color matching tools**: Let Rachel adjust lighting and color across a set of photos so they look cohesive (important for event photography).
- **Skin tone diversity**: Make sure skin smoothing and retouching works across all skin tones. Diversity in product and marketing examples matters.
- **Semi-pro positioning**: Create a messaging angle for Rachel. "Professional-quality touch-ups for family photographers." She's not a professional in the Instagram influencer sense, but she takes the craft seriously.
- **Community for family photographers**: A small, engaged community of people who do similar work (family events, small gatherings) would reinforce Rachel's sense of belonging.
