# The Editing Tool Ecosystem: How Users Stack Apps

No single app owns the photo editing workflow. Users have developed sophisticated, app-specific mental models: native camera for capture, Lightroom for lighting, Facetune for face/body fixes, Canva for design, VSCO for filters. Each tool has a clear job in the chain. Facetune's role is narrow — and that's by design.

## The Stacking Pattern

**Users layer 2-4 apps per photo.** This is universal across Gen Z, male users, and creators. The workflow isn't sequential randomness — it's intentional and repeatable.

Male users consistently follow this pattern:
- **Andrew:** Photos (native) → Lightroom → Facetune
- **Anthony:** Facetune → Snapseed → Lightroom → Canva → West → Huji
- **Cullen:** native iPhone photos → Facetune (for vanish, eyes, patch) → optional Snapseed
- **John:** iPhone → Lightroom → Facetune (for hand redness)

All start with native; all use Lightroom somewhere; all drop in FT for targeted fixes. None use FT as the primary tool.

Gen Z shows the same ecosystem thinking:
- **Caitlin:** VSCO + Peachy for aesthetic consistency
- **Shayda:** Canva for stickers and fun design elements

## Mental Models: What Each App Does

Users have internalized role clarity:
- **Native camera app:** Capture, basic adjustments
- **Lightroom:** Lighting, color, exposure (the heavy lifting)
- **VSCO/Taza:** Filters, aesthetic consistency
- **Facetune:** Face and body fixes (beauty, teeth, eye enlargement, smoothing)
- **Snapseed:** Secondary precision tools
- **Canva:** Graphic design, stickers, composition
- **West/Huji:** Nostalgic filters or special effects

Facetune has been mentally categorized as "face and body only" — not a full editor. Users don't open it for lighting or composition. They open it when they've decided a specific facial feature needs work.

## Churn Study Insight: Where They Go

Churned Gen Z users migrated to:
- **TikTok (60.5%)** — native capture + editing in one platform
- **CapCut (58.5%)** — video editing (indicating shift toward video)
- **VSCO (43.1%)** — full editing + filtering + community

The migration pattern reveals what users value: VSCO is a competitor here because it combines filters, editing, AND community. It's not that VSCO is better at face editing — it's that VSCO is a full ecosystem. Users are consolidating.

## The Implication for Facetune

Facetune's narrow role in the ecosystem is both strength and weakness. **Strength:** Users trust FT for specific face/body tasks. **Weakness:** It doesn't capture the beginning or end of the workflow. Users start elsewhere, finish elsewhere, and call FT for a specific repair job.

This explains why lighting improvements alone won't drive major usage changes. Users have already solved lighting in Lightroom. They're not looking for FT to be a general editor — they're looking for FT to be the face tool that works well within the ecosystem they've already built.
