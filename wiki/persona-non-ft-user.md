# Persona: Non-Facetune User

Non-Facetune users represent the addressable market for growth. They edit photos, they care about quality, but they've either never heard of Facetune or tried it and didn't retain. Understanding what they do, what they value, and why they don't use Facetune is critical for acquisition and messaging strategy.

## Research Overview

This persona is synthesized from two sources:
- **Nov 2025 Survey** (n=536 across 4 demographic segments): Gen Z female, Gen Z male, Millennials, Gen X — all non-Facetune users
- **Nov 2025 Shareout** (n=835): Understanding existing and new users — barriers to adoption

The data reveals universal editing needs across all demographics, demographic-specific feature preferences, awareness gaps, and negative brand associations that are critical to address.

## The Four Segments

### Segment 1: Gen Z Female (n=234)

**Profile**: Young women (18-27), digitally native, active on Instagram and TikTok, edit regularly.

**Editing Priorities (rank order)**:
1. Lighting (top across all groups)
2. Filters/effects
3. Cropping/composition
4. Background editing
5. Color/tone grading

**Facetune awareness**: 44.4% have heard of Facetune; 26.5% tried it in the past (churn risk)

**Why non-users**: Perception that FT = heavy beauty editing. Gen Z females with authenticity concerns avoid it.

### Segment 2: Gen Z Male (n=100)

**Profile**: Young men (18-27), digitally active, gaming interest high, photo editing is secondary to other activities.

**Editing Priorities (rank order)**:
1. Lighting (strongest priority)
2. Filters/effects
3. Cropping/composition
4. **Creative effects** (unique to Gen Z males — #5 for all other groups)
5. Background editing

**Facetune awareness**: Only 41% have heard of Facetune; 59% never heard of it (acquisition opportunity)

**Why non-users**: Low awareness is the primary barrier. Those who know about it see FT as feminine or beauty-focused. Don't see it as relevant to their editing needs.

### Segment 3: Millennials (n=102)

**Profile**: Ages 25-40, less likely to edit regularly, more likely to use phone's native camera app.

**Editing Priorities (rank order)**:
1. Lighting (strong across all age groups)
2. Filters/effects
3. Cropping/composition
4. Background blur
5. Color/tone grading

**Facetune awareness**: ~50% aware; 18% express negative associations ("fake," "catfish," "artificial") — highest negative sentiment of all groups

**Why non-users**: Older users are skeptical of beauty-focused editing apps. They see FT as inauthentic or vain.

### Segment 4: Gen X (n=100)

**Profile**: Ages 41+, less frequent editors, primarily share with family not social media, traditional media consumption.

**Editing Priorities (rank order)**:
1. Lighting (universal need)
2. Cropping/composition
3. Filters/effects
4. Color/tone grading
5. Background editing

**Facetune awareness**: 42% aware; 58% never heard of it (awareness gap)

**Why non-users**: Low engagement with photo editing overall. Those aware of FT see it as not relevant to their use case (family photos, not selfies).

## Universal Insights Across All Groups

### What Everyone Needs

1. **Lighting** — 69-85% across all groups cite lighting as #1 or #2 editing priority. This is the universal editing need.
2. **Filters/effects** — 2nd-4th priority across groups (higher for younger users).
3. **Cropping/composition** — 2nd-5th priority. More valued by older users.
4. **Background editing** — 4th-5th priority for most.

**Critical insight**: The "photo quality" features (lighting, cropping) matter more than "personal beauty" features for non-FT users. This is a positioning gap.

### What Non-Users Don't Care About

1. **Body reshaping** — Ranked LOWEST across all groups (last or near-last)
2. **Face adjustments** (beyond skin smoothing) — Low priority for all groups
3. **Heavy beauty enhancement** — Older groups actively avoid this

**Critical insight**: Non-FT users avoid the features Facetune is known for. The brand is perceived as body/face reshaping tool, but non-users don't want that.

### Generational Differences

- **Gen Z (female)** — Uses filters significantly more (#2 priority) than older groups. Most aligned with creative effects.
- **Gen Z (male)** — Only group with "creative effects" in top 5. Different motivation than female Gen Z; sees editing as photo quality, not beauty.
- **Millennials** — Skeptical of beauty editing. Highest negative sentiment. See FT as fake/inauthentic.
- **Gen X** — Low overall editing frequency. When they edit, it's for photo quality (lighting, cropping). Don't see themselves as target audience.

## Awareness & Brand Perception

### The Awareness Gap

- **Gen Z males**: 59% never heard of Facetune (largest gap)
- **Gen X**: 58% never heard of it
- **Millennials**: ~50% aware (highest)
- **Gen Z females**: 44.4% aware (but 26.5% are churned ex-users)

### Negative Brand Associations (Millennial finding is concerning)

- **12.9% overall** express negative associations ("fake," "catfish," "artificial")
- **Millennials highest** at 18% with negative sentiment
- **Gen X and Gen Z male** lower negative sentiment (but also lower overall awareness)

**Critical insight**: The older you get, the more likely you are to see Facetune as fake or inauthentic. This is a generational messaging challenge.

## Pain Points & Barriers

### For Gen Z Female Non-Users

- **Authenticity concerns**: Like Zoe persona, some Gen Z females avoid FT because of perceived fakeness.
- **Churn from past users** (26.5% tried, didn't retain): They tried it but found it didn't match their editing philosophy or found better alternatives.

### For Gen Z Male Non-Users

- **Awareness**: 59% never heard of it.
- **Perception that FT is for girls**: Gender-coded app perception; males don't see it as relevant.
- **Don't need beauty features**: They want creative effects and photo quality, not face smoothing.

### For Millennial Non-Users

- **Inauthenticity stigma** (18% negative): Older users see heavy editing as dishonest. They value photos as documentary, not art.
- **Not their use case**: Many millennials don't edit much anyway. When they do, it's simple (crop, filter).

### For Gen X Non-Users

- **Low editing frequency overall**: Gen X edits less. Photo editing isn't a core activity.
- **Don't see as relevant**: They edit family photos, not selfies. They want photo quality tools, not beauty tools.

## Competitive Positioning Insights

- **Lighting focus**: Positioning FT as a lighting/photo quality tool (not beauty) would appeal to non-users.
- **De-emphasize body/face reshaping**: These features actually repel non-FT users. Lead with versatile photo enhancement, not body editing.
- **Address authenticity** (for older groups): Messaging that positions editing as photo improvement (like Lightroom) rather than fakeness would help Millennials.
- **Expand creative effects** (for Gen Z): Gen Z cares about effects and filters more than older groups.
- **Target awareness campaigns** (for Gen Z male): 59% haven't heard of FT. Awareness-focused marketing (YouTube, Reddit, gaming communities) would help.
