# Subscription Friction: The Paywall and Trial Problem

The subscription model creates friction at multiple decision points — value clarity at paywall, active workarounds by power users cycling trials, contributor to churn. Users don't believe they're getting value for the price.

## Trial Cycling Behavior

Male FT users (and likely beyond) exploit the 7-day trial by cycling new email addresses. **Cullen and Saintpierre explicitly reported this workaround** — creating throwaway accounts to renew the trial indefinitely rather than subscribe.

This behavior reveals two things:
1. Users see value in FT (they keep using it across trials)
2. Users don't see $X/month value (they won't pay, resort to workarounds instead)

The trial experience is good enough to want continued access, but the subscription paywall is high enough to avoid. The gap is real and systemic.

## Unclear Paywall Value

[[source-onboarding-first-experience]] (n=24) documented that paywall value is not clearly communicated. Users hit the paywall without fully understanding what they unlock. This is a onboarding/UX problem, not a pricing problem.

The issue: FT shows users some features, then paywalls advanced features. But users haven't yet internalized *why* those features matter to their workflow. They see paywall before feeling the need.

## Subscription Contagion

Male users Cullen and John (from [[source-male-first-insights]]) both cancelled Lightroom subscriptions for the same reason: subscription fatigue. They're evaluating multiple subscriptions (FT + Lightroom + Snapseed) and making trade-off decisions. When Facetune paywall is unclear, Lightroom looks more valuable.

This suggests FT competes not just against other editing apps but against the broader subscription ecosystem. Users have a mental budget for creative tools ($X/month max). FT is fighting for space in that budget.

## Pricing as Churn Driver

[[source-gen-z-churn]]: 15.6% of churned Gen Z users cite "too expensive" explicitly. Combined with the alternative adoption (VSCO is freemium, TikTok and CapCut are free), pricing becomes a real friction point for price-sensitive segments.

The finding isn't that FT is expensive in absolute terms — it's that users don't see the value clearly enough to justify the price relative to free alternatives.

## The Critical Window

Friction is highest at two moments:
1. **Paywall (Day 1-3):** Users haven't yet felt need for premium; value is abstract
2. **Trial end (Day 6-8):** Users have used FT but haven't integrated it into workflow; paying feels optional

The [[source-onboarding-first-experience]] study suggests this is where many drop off. The solution isn't cheaper pricing — it's earlier value realization and clearer paywall communication.
