# Source: Exploring Gen X (46-60)

**Researcher:** Shira Kedem | **Date:** April 29, 2026 | **Type:** Shareout (market research + unmoderated usability testing)

Gen X (ages 46-60) is the "bridge generation" — raised analog, living digital. They are tech-savvy, present on every major social platform, and use social media primarily for family connection rather than personal branding. Usability sessions with 9 older FT users revealed they want to look like themselves (just a bit better), value control via the intensity slider, but find the editor menu too complex.

## Market Context

| Stat | Value | Source |
|------|-------|--------|
| Population | 65M Americans born 1965-1980 | U.S. Census Bureau |
| Median household income | $101K (highest of any generation) | Census Bureau |
| Sandwich generation | 42% have both aging parent and a child | Pew Research (2013) |
| Smartphone ownership | 91% (same rate as younger adults) | AARP 2025, n=3,605 |
| Tech makes life easier | 66% say so | AARP 2025 |
| Devices owned | 7 on average (ages 50+) | AARP 2025 |

## Social Media Presence

Ages 50-64 are active across every major platform (Pew 2025, n=5,022):

| Platform | % |
|----------|---|
| YouTube | 85% |
| Facebook | 74% |
| Instagram | 40% |
| TikTok | 30% |

54% of 50-64 year olds use Facebook daily. YouTube is their #1 platform.

## Sharing Motivation: Family, Not Followers

51% use social media primarily to stay in touch with family. Secondary audiences: friends (23%), acquaintances (12%), colleagues (11%), new people (4%). Photos shared are of family events, vacations, milestones — not personal branding.

## Accessibility Barriers

Four categories of barriers affect Gen X adoption (AARP 2025 + NN/g 2024):

| Barrier Type | Examples |
|-------------|----------|
| Visual | Small text, low contrast, light UI |
| Motor | Small tap targets, precise gestures needed |
| Cognitive | Complex navigation, hidden features, overload |
| Trust | Privacy concerns, data use worries, AI skepticism |

## Usability Sessions: What We Heard

**Setup:** 9 participants (8 aged 46-60, 1 aged 71), all Facetune users, recruited via UserTesting. 3 unmoderated tests: Free editing, Looks + Retouch, Specific tasks. Small sample — each group had slightly different task flows due to recruitment difficulty.

### Key Findings

**They want to look like themselves — just a bit better.** Gen X users don't want to look way younger in an unrealistic way. They want to look fresh and natural — a good version of themselves that people will still recognize. This echoes the [[authenticity-and-editing]] pattern seen in Gen Z and male users.

**Users valued the intensity slider.** It gave them control over how far an edit goes and allowed them to pull back before results feel "too much." Multiple participants specifically praised the slider as a key feature.

**The editor menu is too complex and too full of tools.** Users rated the editing tools as easy, but the one usability struggle was navigating the menu. Users found the same function in multiple places and didn't know which one to use.

## Recommendations

1. **They want to look like themselves on a good day** — results that feel fake or unrecognizable break trust. Consider adding "Looks" presets that feel age-appropriate and subtle.
2. **Simple navigation over feature count** — a packed menu with duplicate tools creates confusion. Consider surfacing only the most-used tools for this age group.
3. **Older users need a different UI** — avoid small text, low contrast, and interactions that require precise gestures.

## Methodology

- **Type:** Market research review + unmoderated usability testing
- **Participants:** 9 Facetune users (8 aged 46-60, 1 aged 71)
- **Tests:** 3 unmoderated tests (Free editing, Looks + Retouch, Specific tasks)
- **Platform:** UserTesting.com
- **Limitations:** Small sample; each group had slightly different task flows
- **Date:** April 2026
