# Gen Z Churn Deep Dive: The 6-Month Crisis

61% of Gen Z female users churn within 6 months. The critical insight is that the top three churn drivers are statistically tied at roughly 20% each, not one dominant reason. Understanding this three-way split changes retention strategy.

## The Three Tied Churn Drivers

**1. Authenticity concerns: 20.8%** — users leave because editing feels inauthentic or they fear being perceived as fake

**2. No longer needing face/body editing: 19.6%** — users simply stop caring about beauty edits (shift in priorities, growing confidence, reduced photo activity)

**3. Found better alternatives: 18.4%** — users discovered another app that solves their problem better or more conveniently

Also significant:
- **Too expensive:** 15.6% cite pricing friction
- **Too difficult:** 12.8% find the app too complex for their needs

The three-way tie means you can't solve churn by picking one problem. Retention requires addressing all three simultaneously.

## What They Used Before Leaving

Before churning, the most-used features were:
- **Skin smoothing: 42.8%** — by far the #1 feature, yet these users still left
- **Lighting/color: 38%** — similar pattern; high-use feature among churned users
- **Face adjustments: 31.2%** — reshaping, enlargement, facial tweaks

The paradox: the most-used feature (skin smoothing) is also heavily used by users who then churn. Feature usage ≠ satisfaction or retention.

## The "Too Perfect" Problem

When asked about results, churned users said:
- **38.9% prefer natural outcomes** — they don't want the heavy-filter look anymore
- **34.3% say results look unrealistic** — even when they used the feature, it felt "off" to them

This is a product perception issue, not a capability issue. Facetune's smoothing is effective. Users *used* it. But they perceived the results as too dramatic or fake, contributing to churn. The authenticity tension ([[authenticity-and-editing]]) is directly visible in churn behavior.

## Where They Went

Churned Gen Z users migrated to:
- **TikTok: 60.5%** — native platform capture + editing integration
- **CapCut: 58.5%** — video editing (signals shift toward video content)
- **VSCO: 43.1%** — full editing, community, fresh brand perception

The migration pattern reveals what these users wanted: platforms that integrated editing into content creation (TikTok, CapCut) or multi-purpose editors with lighter filter aesthetics (VSCO).

## Why Alternatives Were Better

Among churned users, reasons alternatives were superior:
- **Easier to use: 25%** — UX friction point
- **Had capabilities they needed: 24%** — product gap (usually lighting, composition, or video)
- **Better lighting/filters: 17%** — confirming [[lighting-universal-editing-need]]
- **Free or low-cost: 17%** — pricing sensitivity

## The Early Churn Window

61% churn in 6 months means the **first 6 months is make-or-break.** Users are evaluating whether FT is worth the subscription price, whether they understand how to use it, and whether the results match their values.

Early intervention is critical:
- Onboarding clarity (why they're paying)
- Demonstrating lighting/filtering capability (not just beauty)
- Establishing authenticity-first product narrative
- Reducing friction in first sessions

Late-stage retention (3-6 months in) is harder because the decision has already calcified.

## The Pricing Factor

15.6% cite "too expensive" explicitly. Combined with the alternative migration (where many found free or cheaper options), pricing is a secondary but meaningful churn vector. Especially for Gen Z, where VSCO (freemium + community), TikTok (free), and CapCut (free) are viable alternatives.
