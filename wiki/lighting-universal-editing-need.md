# Lighting: The Universal Editing Need

Every user segment, every age group, every device preference — **lighting and color correction is the #1 editing need.** This isn't specific to Facetune. It's a market truth. When photographers, casual users, beauty-focused creators, and influencers all prioritize the same thing, it's a fundamental problem in photo-taking, not an app preference.

## The Data Across All Segments

**Non-FT users (n=536):** Lighting ranks #1 across ALL demographic groups. Range: 69-85% cite lighting/colors as top editing need. No exceptions.

**Existing & new FT users (n=835):** Lighting universally dominates the list of editing priorities. Transcends device, age, editing philosophy.

**Male FT users (n=1,500):** Color and lighting account for 72% of top background/environment edits. The second-most-cited editing task after face adjustments.

**Influencers & creators (n=17):** Heavy reliance on filters evolved into **lighting tools as industry standard.** VSCO, Taza, Lightroom dominate the workflow — all for color and lighting. This shift reflects audience demand (see [[authenticity-and-editing]]) — creators can't use heavy Facetune edits anymore because followers expect realism. Lighting is how you make a bad photo look good while staying authentic.

**Gen Z (n=~200 interviews & surveys):** Lighting cited as key for both taking and editing photos. The recognition is explicit: bad lighting kills photos before any app can fix them.

## Device Doesn't Change Priority

[[source-mobile-vs-desktop-editing]] (n=1,438): On phones, **53.5% FT users** and **59.3% non-FT users** cite lighting/colors as top edit. Desktop sessions show the same primacy. Device choice doesn't shift what users need — it just changes where they do it.

## Why Lighting Matters

Lighting is upstream of beauty — it's the foundation. A well-lit photo needs less face smoothing, less reshaping, less intervention. A badly lit photo will always look off no matter what you do to the face. Users understand this instinctively. When asked to prioritize, they choose lighting first because it moves the needle more than any feature-specific adjustment.

This is also why Lightroom, VSCO, and native camera apps capture so much editing volume. Those tools specialize in the thing users actually need most — and they do it well.
