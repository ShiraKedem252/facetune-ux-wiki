# Source: Reactions to Augmented Photography

**Researcher:** Shira | **Date:** (date from study) | **Sample:** n= (from study)

Augmented Photography (AI-generated background, setting, or scene enhancement) triggers uncanny valley responses. User reactions split sharply between "Editing Users" (accepting of AI enhancement) and "Non-Editing Users" (disturbed by artificial content).

## Uncanny Valley Effect

Users report discomfort when AI-generated elements feel "almost real but not quite." Specific trigger points:
- Unnatural lighting
- Inconsistent shadows
- Over-processed edges
- Mismatched perspective

Acceptance increases when the AI effect is clearly stylized (not pretending to be photorealistic) or heavily edited in a recognizable style.

## Editing vs. Non-Editing User Personas

**Editing Users** (primarily Nicole, Jordan, Alex personas)
- Comfortable with "enhanced reality"
- Willing to suspend disbelief
- Accept AI as a creative tool
- Adoption rate: High

**Non-Editing Users** (primarily Zoe, Annie personas)
- Expect authenticity
- Disturbed by artificial elements
- Skeptical of "fake" improvements
- Adoption rate: Low

## Sensitivity Layers Framework

Users have different comfort thresholds depending on use context:

| Layer | Example | Editing Users | Non-Editing Users |
|-------|---------|---|---|
| **Subtle Enhancement** | Slightly better lighting | ✓ Accept | ✓ Accept |
| **Moderate Generation** | Enhanced background blur | ✓ Accept | ✗ Reject |
| **Heavy Generation** | Entirely new background | ✓ Accept | ✗ Reject |
| **Complete Fabrication** | Person in fake location | ✓ Accept | ✗✗ Reject |

## Messaging Implication

Augmented Photography adoption requires **transparency** about what's AI-generated. Users who know a background is fabricated rate it higher than users who suspect it but aren't told.

## Methodology

- **Type:** Qualitative study
- **Methods:** User reactions to AP samples, explanation of features, willingness to use
- **Coverage:** Both editing-forward and photo-purist segments
