# Onboarding: The First Impression Problem

Facetune's onboarding has been studied twice — first in January 2025 (n=24 moderated sessions) and again in April 2026 (n=120 unmoderated usability tests). Together, these studies reveal a consistent pattern: the current onboarding is simple and efficient, but it fails to create emotional connection, communicate app value, or deliver a personalization payoff.

## What Two Studies Tell Us

### Study 1: First Experience (Jan 2025)
The [[source-onboarding-first-experience]] study identified two core barriers: feature discoverability (users couldn't find what the app offered) and paywall friction (hitting a subscription wall before seeing value). New users were confused about what Facetune could do.

### Study 2: Onboarding Revamp (April 2026)
The [[source-onboarding-revamp]] study tested three concepts against each other. The current baseline is praised for simplicity but criticized for low engagement, an uncomfortable gender question, and no visible personalization payoff. The Persona concept (flow-chart navigation with a personality quiz) emerged as the strongest alternative — creating delight and emotional differentiation — though quantitative differences weren't statistically significant.

## The Recurring Themes

**1. Users don't understand what the app does.** Both studies found that onboarding fails to communicate Facetune's capabilities. The 2025 study found discoverability problems. The 2026 study found that even the best concepts didn't clearly showcase editing features.

**2. Questions without payoff erode trust.** The current baseline asks questions (gender, editing goals) but never shows users how those answers change their experience. Users feel like they're completing a survey for the company's benefit, not their own.

**3. Simplicity is necessary but not sufficient.** Users appreciate a short, easy onboarding (the baseline's #1 strength). But "efficient" isn't the same as "engaging" — about a third of users rated the baseline's engagement at 2-3 out of 5.

**4. The gender question is a liability.** Nearly half of participants in the 2026 study called the gender question intrusive, confusing, or irrelevant. The justification copy ("this helps us improve") wasn't convincing.

**5. Delight is possible without adding friction.** The Persona concept proved that a 5-question flow can feel effortless when the interaction format is engaging. Users described it as "a journey" — not a chore.

## Connection to Churn

The [[gen-z-churn-analysis]] found that 61% of Gen Z users churn within 6 months, with 12.8% citing difficulty and 15.6% citing cost. The onboarding-to-paywall sequence is the first critical retention window. If users don't understand what the app does and hit a paywall before seeing value, early churn is predictable.

## Recommendations (Across Both Studies)

1. **Develop the Persona concept further** — it created the most delight and had the tightest variance (users consistently rated it positively). Broaden answer options and make the personality result feel functionally connected to the app.
2. **Show the personalization payoff** — "Based on your answers, we recommend..." closes the loop between questions and value.
3. **Remove or rethink the gender question** — it's creating friction with no clear user-facing benefit.
4. **Delay the paywall** — let users experience core features before asking them to pay. The 2025 study and churn data both point to paywall timing as a retention lever.
5. **Consider hybrid Persona + social proof** — showing "38% of users share your type" adds community without the confusion of the Poll concept's percentage reveals.
